title: "Analog sticks and axes"
description: "Configure dead zones, diagonal range, stick modes, mouse acceleration, and axis throttle for precise analog stick behavior in AntiMicroX."
---

Analog sticks produce continuous axis values rather than simple on/off signals. AntiMicroX exposes several settings to tune how those raw values are interpreted and translated into keyboard, mouse, or other actions.

To access stick settings, click the stick widget in the main window (labeled **LS** or **RS**, or **Left Stick** / **Right Stick**).

## Dead zones

A dead zone is a range of axis values around the center position that AntiMicroX treats as "not moved." Physical analog sticks never return to a perfect center — there is always a small amount of mechanical drift. Without a dead zone, that drift causes unintended input.

### Recommended values

| Controller | Recommended dead zone |
|---|---|
| Xbox 360 / XInput-compatible | **8,000** (XInput standard) |
| Logitech F310 | **2,000** |
| Other controllers | Experiment — lower values if the stick feels unresponsive; raise if there is cursor drift at rest |

<Tip>
  The XInput standard dead zone of 8,000 is a safe starting point for most Xbox-style controllers. For twitchy mouse aiming, try decreasing the right stick dead zone to around 6,000 — this makes movement feel more responsive at the cost of possible slight cursor drift when the stick is released.
</Tip>

<Note>
  If you lower the dead zone for mouse control, monitor whether the mouse cursor drifts when you release the stick. A small amount of drift is usually acceptable and easy to compensate for during gameplay.
</Note>

## Diagonal range

The diagonal range controls how many degrees of arc around each diagonal direction are treated as a diagonal input. Directions outside that arc are treated as a pure cardinal direction.

### For keyboard mappings (left stick)

The default of **45 degrees** is recommended when mapping WASD or other keyboard keys to the left stick in **Standard** stick mode. This gives equal arc to both pure cardinal and diagonal directions.

<Tip>
  If a game overreacts to direction changes — especially when the stick passes through a diagonal zone — enable the **stick delay** setting. Even a value of 0.01 seconds introduces a tiny lag before registering a direction change, which can greatly improve control stability.
</Tip>

### For mouse control (right stick)

A diagonal range of at least **65 degrees** is recommended for mouse movement. Setting it to **89 or 90 degrees** maximizes the portion of stick travel used for diagonal movement and minimizes the narrow arcs reserved for pure cardinal directions, enabling more responsive and "twitchy" cursor movement.

| Use case | Recommended diagonal range |
|---|---|
| Keyboard (WASD movement) | 45° |
| Mouse (general aim) | 65°–75° |
| Mouse (twitchy / FPS aim) | 89°–90° |

## Stick delay

The stick delay setting adds a brief pause before AntiMicroX registers a direction change. This prevents rapid flickering between directions when the stick passes through a diagonal boundary.

- Set to **0** to disable (default)
- Even **0.01 seconds** can meaningfully improve control in games sensitive to rapid direction switches
- Most useful for left stick keyboard mappings; less relevant for mouse control

## Mouse acceleration (right stick)

When the right stick is mapped to mouse movement, AntiMicroX can apply **extra acceleration** to make movement faster when the stick is pushed further. This is configured in the stick's properties dialog.

### Extra duration

The **extra duration** setting has the largest impact on mouse feel. It controls how long the acceleration ramp continues after the stick is pushed. Increasing this value "loosens" the mouse — the cursor accelerates for longer, enabling quick 180° turns while still allowing precision aim at low stick deflections.

### Multiplier

The **multiplier** changes the overall speed scaling applied during the acceleration phase. Raise it to increase the maximum speed reached at full stick deflection.

### Acceleration curves

Four acceleration curve types are available:

| Curve | Behavior |
|---|---|
| **Linear** | Speed increases at a constant rate. The historical default — predictable but can feel uniform across the stick's range. |
| **Ease Out Sine** | Rapid initial acceleration that eases off as you approach maximum deflection. |
| **Ease Out Quad** | Starts fast and tapers to a smooth plateau. Recommended for most FPS-style mouse control. |
| **Ease Out Cubic** | Similar to Ease Out Quad but with a steeper initial burst of acceleration. |

<Tip>
  **Ease Out Quad** is the recommended curve for right-stick mouse movement. Combined with an appropriate extra duration and multiplier, it enables quick 180° turns while keeping precision aim at low stick deflection.
</Tip>

### Mouse movement modes

| Mode | Behavior |
|---|---|
| **Cursor** | Standard cursor movement — the cursor moves proportionally to stick deflection. This is the default. |
| **Spring** | The cursor snaps back to a defined screen position when the stick is released. Useful for applications where the cursor should always return to a fixed point. |

## Axis throttle

Axis throttle lets you remap the usable range of an axis. This is set per-axis from the stick or trigger configuration.

| Throttle type | Effect |
|---|---|
| **Normal** | Full range from minimum to maximum |
| **Positive half** | Only the positive half of the axis range is used |
| **Negative half** | Only the negative half of the axis range is used |
| **Flip** | Inverts the axis direction |

<Note>
  Throttle settings are most commonly needed for trigger axes. Triggers physically travel in only one direction but may be reported as a full bidirectional axis by SDL2. Setting throttle to **Positive half** or **Negative half** maps the trigger to only the portion of the axis where it actually produces values.
</Note>
